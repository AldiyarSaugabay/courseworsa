World Record Shattered: New 100m Benchmark Set at 9.72 Seconds

By Marcus Thompson • November 11, 2025 • 20 minutes ago

In a stunning display of speed and power, the 24-year-old sprinter from Jamaica, Kai 'The Comet' Dixon, obliterated the previous world record for the men's 100 meters, crossing the finish line in an astonishing 9.72 seconds at the Diamond League final in Zurich. The performance, aided by perfect conditionsincluding a tailwind of exactly +1.9 m/s, just under the legal limitand a roaring crowd, marks a new era in men's sprinting.

A Bolt from the Blue: Dixon's Historic Run

Dixon, who had already signaled his world-class potential by running a personal best of 9.80 earlier this season, exploded from the blocks and separated himself from a stacked field that included the reigning Olympic and World Champions. His reaction time of 0.115 was among the fastest of the finalists, giving him an immediate advantage he never relinquished.

The race was a masterclass in acceleration and maximum velocity. Dixon powered through the drive phase and maintained his form impeccably through the crucial final 40 meters. The time of 9.72 seconds officially lowers the previous world standard of 9.74 (set by the legendary 'Jet' Sterling in 2023) by a remarkable 0.02 , a significant margin in the tightly contested world of elite sprinting.

🥈 Noah Lyles (USA) 9.86 (SB)

"I knew I had it in me, but to see 9.72 on the board... it's surreal. This is for my country, for my team, and for everyone who believed this was possible. We are just getting started."
Kai Dixon, New 100m World Record Holder

The Anatomy of a Record

Track analysts were quick to dissect Dixon's performance, highlighting the precision of his execution. Split times indicate that he reached his peak velocity around the 60-meter mark, sustaining an average speed of over 10.3 meters per second for the majority of the race.

Key Performance Metrics:

  • Reaction Time: 0.115
  • 60m Split Time: Approximately 6.30
  • Peak Velocity: approx 42 km/h (or approx 26.1 mph)
  • Final Time: 9.72
  • Previous WR: 9.74

The conditions at the Letzigrund Stadium in Zurich were ideal: a temperature of 22^\circ C, low humidity, and the crucial, maximum-legal tailwind. Race organizers noted that the Mondo track surface was also perfectly prepared for fast times.

Jamaica's Reign in Sprinting Continues

Dixon’s achievement reinforces Jamaica's status as the global epicenter of men's and women's sprinting. He is the latest in a long line of Jamaican speedsters who have dominated the 100m event, following in the footsteps of legends who have held or contended for the world record over the last two decades.

At just 24, Dixon has now moved to the very top of the all-time list, ahead of his nation's predecessors. The immediate question is whether this time is an outlier, or if it represents a new, sustainable level of performance in the sport. Given his youth and technical improvements under coach Ava Sterling, the consensus is that Dixon could be poised for a period of sustained dominance.

"This is the fastest human being on earth right now. What's incredible is his composure; he looked completely relaxed through the finish line. When he corrects the minor stagger in his first 30 meters, he might go lower still. The 9.60s are now officially on the clock."
Michael Johnson, Olympic Gold Medalist and Analyst

Impact on the Global Stage

The record-breaking run sets a scorching pace for the next two major championships: the 2026 World Athletics Championships and the 2028 Olympic Games. Rivals such as the USA's Noah Lyles, who finished second with a season-best 9.86 , now have a new, daunting benchmark to target.

2. Jet Sterling (JAM) 9.74 s

The Road Ahead

Dixon is expected to conclude his season with a few exhibition appearances before taking a well-deserved break. The primary focus for 2026 will be replicating this record-breaking form at the World Championships. For now, however, the athletics world can only marvel at the young Jamaican's achievement. He has delivered one of the most memorable moments in the history of the sport, redefining the limits of human speed and setting the new 100m benchmark at an almost unbelievable 9.72 seconds.

"When you witness a time like this, you remember why you fell in love with athletics. It's pure, unadulterated speed. We are entering the 'Dixon Era.'"
Sebastian Coe, World Athletics President

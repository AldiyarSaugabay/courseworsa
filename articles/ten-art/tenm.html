The Mental Game: Inside a Grand Slam Champion's Mind

By Anna Martinez • November 11, 2025 • 25 minutes ago

In an exclusive interview, the reigning US Open champion, Aryna Sabalenka, opens up about the psychological preparation required to compete at the highest level. From managing pressure on match point to maintaining focus during grueling three-set battles, discover the mental strategies that separate champions from contenders.

The Solitude of the Court

Unlike team sports, tennis players face moments of crisis entirely alone. Sabalenka, known for her powerful game and fiery demeanor, admits that harnessing that energy under pressure was once her greatest challenge.

"My physical game was ready for a Grand Slam win long before my mind was. You are isolated out there. There's no coach's live advice, no teammate to cover a mistake. It's just you and the overwhelming feeling that the entire world is watching your one mistake."
Aryna Sabalenka

Building a Mental Fortress: Attentional Control

Sabalenka discusses how her team introduced "Attentional Control Training"a psychological tactic focused on anchoring the mind to the present moment. This is particularly vital in tennis, where momentum can shift in a single point.

  • The Towel Routine: A five-second sequence used between pointshead down, deep breath, visual cue of the racquet stringsto force a reset.
  • Controlled Breathing: Specific inhale-exhale patterns used during the 25-second shot clock to activate the parasympathetic nervous system and reduce physical tension.
  • The "Just Hit It" Rule: A strategy to prevent cognitive interference, where overthinking leads to passive shots. Her instruction to herself is simply to trust the training and strike the ball with full commitment.

Visualization: Rehearsing the Impossible

The champion reveals the depth of her visualization practices. It's not just imagining lifting the trophy; it’s about rehearsing the most difficult scenarios.

The Anatomy of a Crisis Point

Scenario: Match Point Down Mental Rehearsal: Focus only on the serve toss and contact. Block out the scoreboard.
Scenario: Break Point Up Mental Rehearsal: Treat it as 0-0. Avoid rushing the point. Play aggressive but calculated tennis.

"I spend hours visualizing the worst moments: double faults on set point, missed overheads, the crowd booing a call. I mentally rehearse my response: not frustration, but a calm, immediate shift to the next point. If you’ve practiced that response, it’s automatic when the pressure hits."

Resilience and the Grand Slam Grind

The mental challenge of a Grand Slam is unique because it requires winning seven matches over two weeks, demanding not just skill, but unparalleled emotional regulation and physical recovery.

Anna Martinez pressed Sabalenka on the secret to maintaining motivation through the early rounds when the tension is lower, only to peak for the final.

"My coach calls it 'Achievement Motivation.' We focus on the process, not the outcome. Every practice, every match, is about executing the small goals perfectlylike hitting a target, or getting my first-serve percentage to 65. The final is just a reward for consistently achieving those small goals."

"The greatest psychological victory isn't winning the last point; it's getting comfortable with the fear of losing. Once you accept that fear, you can play free. That's what changed my career."
Aryna Sabalenka

By transforming her internal dialogue and implementing military-grade mental routines, Aryna Sabalenka has cemented her place among the elite. Her story proves that at the top level of tennis, the battle is won not just with a powerful serve, but with an iron will.

F1's Sustainability Drive: Racing Towards Net Zero by 2030

By Rachel Kim • November 11, 2025 • 10 hours ago

How Formula 1 is pioneering green technology in motorsport, from sustainable fuels to carbon-neutral operations, while maintaining its competitive edge. The sport, long criticized for its carbon-intensive global operations, is over halfway to its goal, having delivered a 26% reduction in its carbon emissions since 2018.

The Engine Revolution: 100% Sustainable Fuel

The most significant innovationand the one with the greatest potential impact beyond motorsportis the development of 100% advanced sustainable fuel, mandated for the new-generation hybrid power units starting in 2026.

This is not a traditional biofuel but a "drop-in" synthetic fuel (e-fuel), meaning it can be used in standard road cars without engine modification. It is created using non-food bio-sources, genuine waste, or carbon captured directly from the air. Crucially, the goal is to make the fuel carbon neutralthe amount of emitted when burned is balanced by the removed from the atmosphere during its production.

"While the race cars themselves account for less than 1% of F1's total carbon footprint, the sustainable fuel technology we are developing has the potential to decarbonize sectors like aviation and shipping. F1 is a lab for the world’s transportation future."
Stefano Domenicali, President & CEO of Formula 1

Decarbonizing the Global Logistical Chain

Travel and freight account for the largest share of F1's carbon emissions. To tackle this, the sport has implemented sweeping changes in its logistics and operations:

Strategy Pillars for Net Zero by 2030:

  • Ultra-Efficient Logistics: Upgrading freight containers to allow for the use of more efficient Boeing 777 aircraft. There is also an increased shift towards biofuel trucks for European races and a greater reliance on sea freight where feasible.
  • Remote Operations: Expanding remote broadcast centers in the UK. This reduces the amount of technical equipment and roughly 140 personnel who need to travel to every race, resulting in a 25% reduction in travel emissions.
  • Calendar Alignment: Working to regionalize the race calendar starting in 2026 to group races geographically (e.g., North America cluster, Middle East cluster) and reduce transcontinental flights.

Factory to Flag: Renewable Energy and Event Sustainability

The drive extends to the home bases and the race venues themselves. Factories and facilities across F1 and its teams have seen the biggest success story in percentage terms, with emissions cut by 59% compared to 2018.

  • Renewable Facilities: F1’s offices now run on 100% renewable energy, and the teams' energy-intensive factories are increasingly powered by sustainable sources like solar and wind.
  • Sustainable Events: Guidance has been issued to all race promoters to eliminate single-use plastics, promote waste reuse/recycling, and provide incentives for fans to use public transport or bicycles (like at Zandvoort).
  • Green Power at Events: F1 is rolling out innovative, low-carbon energy generation systems at race weekends, using HVO biofuel and battery storage instead of traditional diesel generators, leading to a significant drop in event operations emissions.

As F1 races toward the 2030 finish line, its success in balancing extreme performance with environmental responsibility aims to provide a powerful, high-visibility blueprint for global industries and other major sports to follow.
